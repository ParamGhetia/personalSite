## Capitalism's Role 
Design being so integral to our daily life means that its perception can be drastically altered. This is what John Berger suggests in Ways of Seeing, writing “our ways of seeing are shaped by what we know or believe.” meaning that our perception is conditioned by surrounding systems rather than what is objectively there. 
If perception is a child of social and economic construct, that would imply the dominance of capitalism does not only influence the products designed but also how design itself is perceived and the value placed upon it. 

Our economic system is formed in a way that concentrates power and profit in the hands of a few individuals of corporations, shaping not only what is produced, but how it is designed. Because these companies want the utmost growth and profit, their priority is not the quality of experience but the efficiency and profitability of production. This misalignment of motivations sacrifices design and the creatives that work in these companies. They’re forced to work under strict deadlines, financial constraints, and avert risk. As a result, design becomes standardized and optimized for efficiency, replacing the experimentation and aesthetic depth for efficiency and conservativity. 

For these larger companies, design is only justified when it directly contributes to revenue or can reduce costs. If neither of these are accomplished, these executives treat it as an unnecessary expanse, completely out of touch with the product itself. Our economic system permits executives to make these large decisions about their products despite the fact that they are so far detached from the actual manufacturing and creation of these products. Because of this, creatives are forced to make every visual or structural decision empirically defensible in terms of return on investment rather than _____. 

This creates a very strong selection pressure on design itself. The more eccentric and risky a design is, the less likely it is to appeal to a mass audience. In the context of capitalism, it becomes less viable and valuable. Thus, companies gravitate towards safer, more familiar and conservative design choices. What actually emerges is not the best design in an artistic sense but just the most profitable one, one that can bring the executives the greatest monetary value.

This effect doesn't just exist isolated in a single company but it spreads across the entire market. Another attribute of capitalism is the advocacy for competition. Oftentimes people call for the competition in capitalism to be the source of innovation but this is far from the case. In fact, competitive markets incentivize convergence rather than divergence. Once a company proves that a design strategy is profitable, all other firms will be quick to replicate it, reducing any variety and discouraging aesthetic experimentation in fear of the uncertainty that it holds. Companies are too scared to veer from the norm, scared that they will quickly be crushed by the competition if they let up. The phone industry is the perfect example of this behavior. As one company hones a specific structural feature that proves successful, all the others jump on the bandwagon and it shortly becomes an industry standard.

You can see this behavior here with the Galaxy on the bottom soon adopting the rounded corners and the iPhone on the top adopting the same camera bump as the Galaxy.

My points are best exemplified by the video game industry. Although there are still some companies that care and create great videogames, the majority of the masterfully crafted, visual wonders that emerge from the industry are created by individuals and small teams.
