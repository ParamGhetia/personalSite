## What is Design?  
Our most powerful sense is sight, the sense that delivers the largest amount of information and persists regardless of time. Where other mediums like sound take place over time, visuals are a passive experience. 

Nearly everything we interact with on a daily basis has a visual component, the shape of the desk I’m writing this on, the browser you are viewing this on, the shape and color of these words; they all have visual components. 

This visual component is best known as design. As Bruno Munari explains in Design as Art, “What then is this thing called Design if it is neither style nor applied art? It is planning: the planning as objectively as possible of everything that goes to make up the surroundings and atmosphere in which men live today.” Design is more than just decoration, its the structural force that defines the items and environments that surround us. Design isn’t optional or secondary. It’s embedded into every object and space we encounter. Treating design as a mere byproduct of function and use case would be more than just a disservice to the medium but an offront to the human experience. 

Design has 2 main forms: industrial and graphic. Graphic design refers to the visual element of a two-dimensional space, and industrial design refers to the visual element of something that occupies three-dimensional space. 

In our current society, the way the average person interacts with design is through products: Creations made for the sake of purchase by an individual or group of individuals (company). In these, design takes a backseat to the function of the product. Innovation in the form of the creation is an afterthought to the function.

In the pursuit of the most efficient function and the highest profit, innovation and risk in the form of the form is foregone.
The visuals of a product could do so much more than be the shell for the function of the product. I argue that the form is just as important as the function. 
Alain de Botton agrees with this in his book The Architecture of Happiness. 

“We seem divided between an urge to override our senses and numb ourselves to our settings and a contradictory impulse to acknowledge the extent to which our identities are indelibly connected to, and will shift along with, our locations. An ugly room can coagulate any loose suspicions as to the incompleteness of life, while a sun-lit one set with honey-coloured limestone tiles can lend support to whatever is most hopeful within us."
